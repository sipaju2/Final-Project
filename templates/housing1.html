Housing, Sweet Housing


"The way we create housing isn't working for many Americans."

The American housing market shows a sharp divide of haves and have-nots. Inequality is widening as competition remains fierce for scant resources. Finding housing is getting harder for those of lesser means, but maybe a tiny bit better for everyone else. Homeownership provides a bit of an economic edge for those who can grab it, those being ones who already have well means. More and more, however, it seems the way we create housing isn't working for many, if not increasingly most, Americans.



"Prices can keep going up as long as the growing demand remains unsatiated."

"The duration of today's tight conditions is unprecedented," the researchers said. Because housing is tight, it seems that prices can keep going up as long as the growing demand stays unsatiated. It's not just that there's not building enough to keep up with population and household growth, there's not even building enough to keep up with homes becoming obsolete. The Joint Center estimates that there was a shortfall of about 260,000 units in 2018, and that construction has barely kept pace with household growth for eight years. That tracks with other estimates of a housing shortfall in the millions.





All Work and No House



More higher-income people are renting. Harvard researchers say there were 311,000 more tenant households earning $75,000 or more in 2018 than in the year before. Thatâ€™s great for developers and landlords, but not so hot for households that need lower-cost rents. As the researchers note, "The focus of new construction on higher-cost units has thus shifted the overall distribution of rents upward." Over just one year from 2016-2017, the supply of newly-built homes renting for less than $800 fell by 1 million, and those units aren't being replenished. In the first quarter of 2018, the researchers say, only 9% of apartments completed had asking rents below $1,050.

And worse, rent hasn't gotten any cheaper. With all these conditions in mind, it's no wonder that more American renters are "cost-burdened." One-fifth of households that rent are paying between 30% and 50% of their income on housing costs, while one-quarter are paying more than 50%.



  • More higher-income people are renting
  • Focus has shifted to building higher-cost units
  • Rent has only been increasing
